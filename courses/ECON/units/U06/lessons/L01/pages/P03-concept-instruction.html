Concept | International Trade Basics

International trade is the buying and selling of goods and services between countries. Trade connects producers and consumers in different parts of the world.

An import is a good or service a country buys from another country. For example, if the United States buys coffee grown in another country, that coffee is an import.

An export is a good or service a country sells to another country. For example, if a country sells airplanes, computers, or crops to buyers in another nation, those products are exports.

Countries often trade because they have different resources, climates, skills, and industries. Not every country produces every good efficiently, so trade helps countries get products they do not make as easily on their own.

Specialization means focusing on producing certain goods or services. When a country specializes, it puts more time and resources into the products it can produce well.

Trade can benefit countries by increasing access to goods, expanding consumer choice, and allowing producers to reach more markets. At a basic level, international trade is about exchange, connection, and productive focus.

Think: If one country grows bananas easily and another country builds cars efficiently, why might trade help both countries?

Common mistake: Confusing imports and exports. Imports are bought from another country. Exports are sold to another country.
