Concept | Economic Systems in a Global Context

An economic system is the way a society organizes how goods and services are produced, distributed, and consumed. Different countries use different systems to make economic decisions.

In a market economy, individuals and businesses make many decisions about what to produce, what to buy, and how to set prices. Private choice plays a major role.

In a command economy, the government makes many major economic decisions. It may decide what goods are produced, how resources are used, and how output is distributed.

A mixed economy combines features of both systems. In many countries, markets guide much economic activity while governments also regulate, provide services, or influence certain sectors.

Countries with different economic systems can still trade and participate in global markets. This is why economic systems can be compared across nations in an international setting.

Comparing systems helps explain why countries may organize businesses, government involvement, and economic choices differently. It also shows that most real economies are more complex than a single label suggests.

Think: If a government controls most major industries, which type of economic system does that suggest?

Common mistake: Assuming every country fits perfectly into one category. Many countries use mixed systems with features from more than one model.
