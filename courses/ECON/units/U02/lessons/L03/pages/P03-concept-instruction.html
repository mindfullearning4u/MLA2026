Concept | Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium happens when the quantity consumers want to buy is equal to the quantity producers want to sell. At this point, the market is balanced. There is no pressure for the price to rise or fall because buyers and sellers are in agreement.

The equilibrium price is the market price where this balance occurs. The equilibrium quantity is the amount bought and sold at that price.

If the market price is set above equilibrium, producers want to sell more than consumers want to buy. This creates a surplus. When sellers cannot sell all they produce, they may lower prices.

If the market price is set below equilibrium, consumers want to buy more than producers want to sell. This creates a shortage. When buyers compete for too few goods, prices may rise.

Markets can also change because of shifts in demand or shifts in supply. If demand increases, the equilibrium price usually rises and the equilibrium quantity usually rises. If demand decreases, the equilibrium price usually falls and the equilibrium quantity usually falls.

If supply increases, the equilibrium price usually falls and the equilibrium quantity usually rises. If supply decreases, the equilibrium price usually rises and the equilibrium quantity usually falls.

It is important to remember that movement along a curve is caused by a change in the good's own price. A shift of a curve is caused by another factor, such as changes in income, technology, costs, or number of buyers.

🧠 Think: If more people want a product but the amount available stays the same, what will likely happen to the market price?

⚠️ Common mistake: Confusing a shortage with low supply only. A shortage means quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied at the current price.
