Concept | Market Structures

A market structure describes how a market is organized based on the number of sellers, the type of product, and the level of competition. Different market structures affect how businesses set prices and how much choice consumers have.

In perfect competition, there are many sellers offering identical products. No single business can control the price, so prices are determined by supply and demand. An example might be farmers selling the same type of crop.

A monopoly exists when one seller controls the entire market. This seller has significant power over pricing because there are no close substitutes. Examples may include utilities such as electricity providers in certain regions.

An oligopoly consists of a few large firms that dominate the market. These firms are interdependent, meaning each firm’s decisions affect the others. Industries like airlines or automobile manufacturers often operate as oligopolies.

Monopolistic competition involves many sellers offering similar but not identical products. Businesses compete through branding, quality, and features rather than just price. Restaurants and clothing brands are common examples.

The level of competition in a market affects pricing power. In highly competitive markets, businesses have little control over price. In less competitive markets, businesses have more influence over pricing.

Market structures also affect consumer choice. More competition generally leads to more options and potentially lower prices. Less competition can limit choices and lead to higher prices.

🧠 Think: Would you expect prices to be higher in a monopoly or in perfect competition? Why?

⚠️ Common mistake: Assuming all markets operate the same way. The number of sellers and level of competition change how prices are set.
