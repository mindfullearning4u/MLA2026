Concept Instruction

Demand refers to how much of a product consumers are willing and able to buy at different prices. In economics, demand is connected directly to consumer behavior because buyers make decisions based on price, preferences, income, and expectations.

The law of demand states that when the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded usually falls. When the price falls, the quantity demanded usually rises. This inverse relationship is one of the most basic patterns in economics.

For example, if movie ticket prices increase, some people may decide not to go to the movies as often. If ticket prices decrease, more people may decide that going to the movies is affordable and worth it.

It is important to separate a movement along the demand curve from a shift in demand. A movement along the demand curve happens when the price of the good itself changes. A shift in demand happens when something other than price changes consumer behavior.

Major factors that can shift demand include:

  • Income: if consumers earn more money, they may buy more of some goods.
  • Preferences: if a product becomes more popular, demand may increase.
  • Prices of related goods: substitutes and complements can affect demand.
  • Expectations: if consumers expect prices to rise later, they may buy more now.
  • Number of buyers: more buyers in the market can increase demand.

A substitute is a product that can be used instead of another product. For example, if the price of one brand of sneakers rises, some consumers may buy a different brand instead.

A complement is a product used together with another product. For example, if the price of game consoles falls and more people buy them, demand for video games may increase as well.

🧠Think: If the price of your favorite fast-food meal increases, would you still buy it as often?

⚠️Common mistake: Confusing a change in quantity demanded with a shift in demand. Price changes move along the demand curve. Other factors shift the curve.
