Concept | Inflation and Purchasing Power

Inflation is a general rise in prices over time. When inflation happens, the cost of many goods and services increases across the economy.

Purchasing power means how much your money can buy. If prices rise but your income stays the same, your purchasing power falls because each dollar buys less than before.

For example, if a gallon of milk cost $3 in the past but now costs $4, the same amount of money buys less milk than it did before. The same idea applies to groceries, rent, transportation, and other daily expenses.

Inflation matters because it affects the cost of living. If housing, food, and other necessities become more expensive, families may need more income just to maintain the same standard of living.

Economists study price changes over time to understand how inflation affects households and businesses. Moderate inflation may be manageable, but rapid inflation can make budgeting and planning much harder.

Inflation does not mean every price changes by the same amount. Some prices may rise faster than others, and some may stay more stable for a period of time.

Think: If the price of groceries rises but your paycheck stays the same, how does that affect what you can buy each week?

Common mistake: Thinking inflation means only one item got more expensive. Inflation refers to a broader increase in prices across the economy.
