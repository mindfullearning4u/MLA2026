Concept | Economic Growth and Business Cycles

Economic growth means an economy is producing more goods and services over time. Growth usually reflects greater economic activity and can improve living standards if it continues over time.

Economies do not grow at the same speed all the time. Instead, they move through a business cycle, which is the pattern of rising and falling economic activity.

During an expansion, production, consumer spending, and business activity generally increase. Businesses may open new locations, produce more goods, and hire more workers because demand is strong.

The peak is the highest point of the cycle before activity begins to slow down. After the peak, the economy may begin to contract.

A recession is a period of declining economic activity. During a recession, spending and production often fall, and many businesses may reduce output or delay expansion plans.

The trough is the lowest point of the business cycle. It marks the end of the downturn and the point before improvement begins.

A recovery happens when the economy begins to improve after a recession. Businesses start producing more again, spending increases, and economic activity rises.

These phases matter because they show that the economy changes over time. People, businesses, and governments often watch the business cycle to understand what is happening and to make better decisions.

Think: Why might families and businesses spend more confidently during an expansion than during a recession?

Common mistake: Thinking a recession is the same as the entire business cycle. A recession is only one phase of the cycle.
