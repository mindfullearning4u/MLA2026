Concept | Unemployment and Labor Markets

Unemployment refers to people in the labor force who are willing and able to work but cannot find jobs. Economists study unemployment because it helps show how well a labor market is functioning.

A labor market is where workers offer their labor and employers demand workers. In this market, wages, job openings, worker skills, and business needs all affect hiring.

There are several major types of unemployment. Frictional unemployment happens when people are between jobs or entering the workforce for the first time. For example, a student who graduates and is searching for a first full-time job may experience frictional unemployment.

Structural unemployment happens when workers do not have the skills needed for available jobs or when technology changes the kinds of jobs businesses need. For example, if a factory uses automation and needs fewer assembly workers, some workers may need retraining.

Cyclical unemployment happens when the economy slows down and businesses reduce hiring or lay off workers. When consumers and businesses spend less, companies often need fewer employees.

Workforce trends describe how jobs and worker patterns change over time. New technology, education levels, remote work, and changes in consumer demand can all affect the labor market.

Understanding unemployment and labor markets helps explain why some workers find jobs quickly while others face longer challenges. It also helps show why training, education, and economic conditions matter in the job market.

Think: Why might a worker need new skills even if that person has worked for many years?

Common mistake: Thinking all unemployment is caused by the same problem. Different types of unemployment have different causes.
