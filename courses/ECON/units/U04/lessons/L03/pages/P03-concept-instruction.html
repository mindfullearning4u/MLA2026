Concept | Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation decisions to influence the economy. Governments may use fiscal policy when they want to encourage growth or reduce excessive economic activity.

One part of fiscal policy is government spending. When government spends money on roads, schools, or public projects, that spending becomes part of economic activity.

Another part of fiscal policy is taxation decisions. Governments may decide to lower taxes so households and businesses keep more money, or raise taxes to reduce spending in the economy.

Expansionary fiscal policy is used to encourage economic activity. This may happen when government increases spending, lowers taxes, or does both. The goal is to support spending, production, and jobs.

Contractionary fiscal policy is used to slow economic activity. This may happen when government reduces spending, raises taxes, or does both. The goal is to reduce excessive demand or help limit economic pressure.

Fiscal policy matters because government decisions can affect how much money moves through the economy. Changes in spending and taxes can influence consumer behavior, business activity, and overall economic conditions.

Fiscal policy is not the same as naming types of taxes. In this lesson, the main focus is how governments use spending and tax decisions as a strategy to influence the economy.

🧠 Think: If the economy slows down, why might government decide to spend more money on public projects?

⚠️ Common mistake: Confusing fiscal policy with any tax discussion. Fiscal policy is about how government uses spending and taxation decisions to influence economic activity.
