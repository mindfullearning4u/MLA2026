Concept Instruction

Federalism is the system in which government power is divided between the national government and state governments. Instead of placing all authority in one place, the Constitution distributes power across levels of government.

Delegated powers are powers given to the national government. Examples include printing money, conducting foreign policy, and providing national defense. Reserved powers are powers kept by the states. Examples include running public schools, conducting local elections, and issuing driver licenses.

Concurrent powers are powers shared by both the national and state governments. Examples include collecting taxes, creating courts, and enforcing laws. Federalism allows both levels of government to function, but in different areas and for different responsibilities.

🧠Think: Why might the Constitution divide power instead of giving all power to one government?

⚠️Common mistake: Do not confuse delegated powers with concurrent powers. Delegated powers belong only to the national government, while concurrent powers are shared by both levels of government.
