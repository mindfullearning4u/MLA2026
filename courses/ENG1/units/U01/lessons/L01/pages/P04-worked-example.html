💡Worked Example

💡Read the short passage and watch the active reading thinking process step by step.

Passage: Many students think reading is finished when they reach the last line. Strong readers work differently. They underline important ideas, write questions when something is unclear, and note when a sentence changes their understanding. These actions create a record of thinking that makes it easier to explain what the text means later.

Step 1: Annotate the important idea.
I underline: "They underline important ideas, write questions when something is unclear, and note when a sentence changes their understanding."
Reasoning: This line names the actions that define active reading.

Step 2: Ask a question.
I write in the margin: How does writing questions help comprehension?
Reasoning: I understand the list of actions, but I want to clarify why one of those actions matters.

Step 3: Track thinking.
I write: At first I thought reading was only about finishing. Now I see that strong reading includes visible thinking while reading.
Reasoning: This note records how my understanding changed after reading the passage.

✅Why this works:

  • The annotation identifies a key line instead of marking random text.
  • The question shows the reader is monitoring meaning while reading.
  • The thinking note proves the reader can describe how understanding develops.
