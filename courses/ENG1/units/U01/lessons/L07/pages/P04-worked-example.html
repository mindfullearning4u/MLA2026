💡Worked Example

💡Watch how a reader notices confusion, uses earlier Unit 01 skills, and applies one fix-up strategy.

Passage: The principal described the new schedule as flexible, but the paragraph also said students had fewer choices about when to attend support periods. That wording made the explanation seem inconsistent.

Step 1: Notice the confusion.
I underline flexible and write: This does not match “fewer choices.”
Reasoning: Active reading helps me mark the exact place where the text stops making sense.

Step 2: Identify the problem type.
I decide the confusion comes from word meaning and from the paragraph's main point.
Reasoning: If flexible means having options, the next idea seems to conflict with it.

Step 3: Choose a fix-up strategy.
I reread the sentence and use context clues. Then I restate the central idea of the paragraph: The schedule changes how support time works, but it may not give students more freedom.
Reasoning: Rereading and checking the central idea help me see that the paragraph questions whether the schedule is truly flexible.

✅Why this works:

  • The reader identifies the exact point of confusion instead of saying only “I do not get it.”
  • The response combines active reading, vocabulary in context, and central idea thinking.
  • The chosen strategy matches the specific comprehension problem.
