Concept Instruction

Financial decisions are rarely neutral. When a person chooses one option, another option is not chosen. That is why financial choices should be evaluated carefully instead of made at random.

A strong financial decision includes three questions:

  • What are my options?
  • What do I gain from each option?
  • What do I give up by choosing one option over another?

The idea of opportunity cost helps explain this process. If a student spends $40 on entertainment, that same $40 cannot also be used for savings or a needed purchase.

Financial consequences can be:

  • Short-term: less money available this week
  • Long-term: slower progress toward a savings goal

Good decision-making requires evidence. A person should compare cost, value, timing, and future impact before deciding.

⚠️Common mistake: choosing the most exciting option without checking its future effect on goals or needs.
